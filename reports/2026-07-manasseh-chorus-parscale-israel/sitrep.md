## Bottom line up front (BLUF)

TIME reported on 13 July 2026 that the global advertising agency Havas hired Brad Parscale's firm Clock Tower X in September 2025 to run a digital campaign on behalf of the State of Israel, according to Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA) filings that TIME reviewed. Under the agreement, Parscale's operation would produce at least 100 pieces of content a month, most of it aimed at Gen Z audiences on TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and podcasts, amplify the messaging through social media and Salem Media Network channels, deliver at least 50 million monthly impressions, and work to influence how AI tools including OpenAI's ChatGPT, Anthropic's Claude, and Google's Gemini characterize Israel and the war. Israel agreed to pay $1.5 million a month. The public framing was combating online antisemitism; an Israeli Foreign Ministry official told TIME the strategic aim was to stop young conservatives from turning against Israel.

Aunoo tracks this operation as MANASSEH CHORUS. Aunoo assesses that it combined a disclosed, FARA-registered contract with allegedly undisclosed downstream amplification. TIME reported, citing three people familiar with the campaign, that a network of interconnected Parscale firms (Clock Tower X, Campaign Nucleus, and Influenceable) fed conservative influencers suggested language through private group chats and compensated them by the reach they generated, producing what looks like an organic grassroots conversation. Parscale confirms the campaign existed and was meant to keep young conservatives from drifting from Israel, but denies undermining President Trump and says none of the FARA-registered money paid influencers.

The significance is twofold. First, this is a foreign government paying an American political operator to shape the views of the U.S. President's own political base, a target a U.S. intelligence official told TIME "can't be dismissed as inconsequential." Second, the contract documents a deliberate effort to shape commercial AI systems, both by trying to influence how chatbots describe Israel and by building websites designed to be ingested by those systems. This is one of the most unusual documented terms in the filing and a rare on-the-record example of influence work aimed at the AI information layer.

## What is documented

The account below is drawn from TIME's investigation and the FARA filings it reviewed, plus on-record statements from Parscale and from Israeli and U.S. officials speaking to TIME.

The contract. In September 2025, Havas engaged Clock Tower X for the State of Israel. The FARA filing sets out the terms: 100 original content pieces a month, at least 80 percent aimed at Gen Z across TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and podcasts; amplification across social media and, in a draft Services Agreement, "integration of narrative messaging into Salem Media Network properties and aligned distribution channels"; at least 50 million monthly impressions; and an effort to shape how ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini characterize Israel and the war. The price is $1.5 million a month.

Who Parscale is. Brad Parscale ran the digital operation for Trump's 2016 campaign and was campaign manager for the 2020 re-election effort until he was removed that summer. He now operates Clock Tower X and affiliated firms, including Campaign Nucleus and Influenceable, in which he holds a minority stake, and serves as Chief Strategy Officer at Salem Media Network, a Christian conservative broadcaster. TIME reports that Parscale has not spoken to Trump in more than five years, despite an Israeli official's account that he presented himself as uniquely connected to Trump's world.

The mechanism. Three people familiar with the campaign describe a messaging operation run through Parscale's interconnected firms in which conservative influencers receive suggested language through private group chats and are compensated based on the impressions and engagement their posts generate. Clock Tower X's own website describes an "influencer ecosystem" with "managed networks that amplify narratives through credible, distributed voices." TIME reviewed internal messages from a separate Influenceable campaign offering influencers a base of $2,250 plus $1 per 1,000 views up to two million views, or as much as $4,250 a post. A March 2026 example became public when the conservative figure Nick Sortor accused Influenceable of running an undisclosed campaign for the soda industry, after multiple MAGA influencers posted near-identical messages defending sugary drinks.

The AI-seeding component. Parscale's plan included websites such as PaxPoint.org and FactSignal.org that, per TIME, were designed less for human readers than for AI systems synthesizing information from across the web, a technique meant to shape what chatbots say about Israel.

Parscale's response. Parscale confirms the operation was intended to keep young conservatives from drifting from Israel, but says neither he nor his firms worked to turn opinion against Trump's objectives, calling that claim false. He says none of the FARA-registered contract money was used to pay influencers, because that would require them to disclose the funding source, and that other Christian organizations, which he declined to name, hired his firms to support Israel after 7 October. He also says his contract does not influence Salem hosts, citing FCC rules on foreign influence over broadcast programming.

## Positions of the parties

**Brad Parscale / Clock Tower X.** Parscale confirms the campaign and its goal of retaining young-conservative support for Israel, denies any effort to undermine Trump, and says the FARA-registered funds did not pay influencers. He argues the pro-Israel voices involved already held those views and that his contract does not touch Salem's hosts.

**The State of Israel.** Netanyahu's office declined to comment. An Israeli Foreign Ministry official, speaking anonymously, confirmed the strategic aim and expressed frustration that support for Israel kept eroding despite the spending, saying "we are pissed at Brad Parscale."

**The Trump administration.** A White House spokesperson declined to comment and said they were unaware of the campaign. Officials cited by TIME were frustrated that an effort to keep the right pro-Israel had, in their view, evolved into an influence campaign colliding with the President's own aims, led by a figure trading on a perception of closeness to Trump that TIME reports is outdated.

**U.S. intelligence.** A senior U.S. intelligence official told TIME the notable feature was the target, the President's own base, and warned that "if there's one Brad Parscale out there, there are others."

## Update — 15 July 2026 (JD Vance remarks)

On 15 July 2026, Vice President JD Vance said on *The Joe Rogan Experience* that people within the Israeli government were attempting to change American public opinion in order to prolong the conflict with Iran, describing a "very discreet, extremely well-funded campaign." During the interview he referred to TIME's reporting on the Parscale campaign, and said the online attacks had also targeted him personally over the U.S.–Iran deal. His comments provide on-record evidence of concern at the highest level of the U.S. administration, but do not independently establish that Clock Tower X directed the posts identified by officials, or that Israeli contract funds were used to pay influencers.

## Implications (second-order effects)

The first effect concerns disclosure and domestic political integrity. A foreign government is paying an American operator to shape the views of the U.S. President's political base, and the messaging reaches audiences through influencers who present as independent voices. Even with a FARA registration on the contract, the paid, coordinated nature of the downstream amplification is not visible to the audiences receiving it, which is the core transparency problem.

The second effect is the targeting of the AI information layer. The operation does not only aim at human audiences; the contract specifies an effort to shape how the major AI assistants describe Israel, and the operation built sites intended to be read by those systems rather than by people. It raises an enforcement question for the AI providers, whose models are the target here rather than the tool.

The third effect is the blurring of covert and overt influence. MANASSEH CHORUS suggests that a registered, disclosed contract can sit alongside allegedly undisclosed downstream amplification. That combination, if borne out, is harder to police than either a clearly covert network or a clearly labeled advertisement.

## Significance

This is a case of state-funded, professionally-run influence work directed at a democracy's own domestic political movement, reportedly amplified through paid creators rather than covert accounts, and reaching toward the AI systems that increasingly mediate public understanding. What makes it distinctive is the structure: a disclosed, registered contract combined with allegedly undisclosed downstream amplification.

**Assessment.** The existence of the contract and its terms are well supported by TIME's review of the FARA filings and by Parscale's own confirmation of the campaign. The paid-influencer mechanism rests on three anonymous sources plus Clock Tower X's own marketing language and a documented prior example. The claim that the operation worked against Trump's ceasefire is contested and denied by Parscale. **Confidence: high on the contract and its stated deliverables; moderate on the scale and effect of the influencer amplification; low on the disputed anti-Trump effect.**
