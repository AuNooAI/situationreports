# SITUATION REPORT: Pro-AI Influence Operations and the "Foreign Influence" Counter-Narrative Against Data-Center Opposition

**Classification:** Unclassified · Open-source intelligence (OSINT)
**As of:** 2 July 2026, 12:00 UTC
**Prepared from:** Aunoo news-intelligence (Disinformation & Influence Operations topic; AI & Machine Learning topic), supplemented by named primary reporting
**Subject:** A pro-AI influence environment that funded paid influencers and China-threat messaging, and the parallel "foreign influence" counter-narrative that reframes opposition to AI data centers as China-linked activity

---

## Bottom line up front (BLUF)

Two influence campaigns are running around the same policy fight over American AI infrastructure. The first is a pro-AI campaign that WIRED documented on 1 May 2026, in which a dark-money group called Build American AI, tied to the pro-AI super PAC Leading the Future, paid social-media influencers to promote American AI and present China as the threat. Related reporting alleges a wider influence stack around the same political network, including an automated pseudo-news site staffed by AI "reporters" and bot-like amplification of pro-AI content on X. The second campaign is a counter-narrative built on reports from the Bitcoin Policy Institute (BPI) and the advocacy group Power the Future, which argue that opposition to AI data centers is being driven by a China-linked foreign influence operation.

The evidence is asymmetric. The pro-AI influencer campaign is well documented, with named actors and campaign materials, alongside responses from implicated parties. The claim that anti-data-center opposition is foreign-driven is not supported by the available evidence. OpenAI's own June 2026 report found a likely China-origin operation testing anti-data-center messaging but recorded no meaningful breakout, WIRED reported that Graphika had not seen evidence of a scaled foreign operation, and one analysis found that a majority of data-center opposition is led by Republicans rather than any foreign actor.

The most defensible interpretation is that the BPI material functions as the policy-paper layer of a pro-AI counter-campaign. It supplies an evidentiary-looking frame that recasts local opposition to data centers as a national-security and foreign-influence problem, and lawmakers and investors are already citing it for that purpose. There is not enough public evidence to show that BPI is directly funded by, coordinated with, or operationally part of the Build American AI or Leading the Future network. The safer claim throughout is that pro-AI political and advocacy infrastructure, rather than AI firms acting as corporate entities, was exposed conducting these operations.

## What happened

The pro-AI influence campaign was reported first. On 1 May 2026, WIRED reported that Build American AI, a nonprofit tied to Leading the Future, funded a coordinated social-media campaign using lifestyle and political influencers to promote American AI, frame China as the threat, and push "Team USA" messaging. Influencers were pitched deals reported at around $5,000 per TikTok video. A campaign staffer quoted by WIRED described the aim as subtly shifting public debate by framing China's AI advancement as a serious risk to Americans. OpenAI and Palantir denied corporate affiliation with, or corporate funding of, Leading the Future and Build American AI.

The campaign sits inside a large pro-AI political-finance network. Axios reported in January 2026 that Leading the Future had raised more than $125 million to influence the 2026 midterms and federal AI regulation, with contributors including OpenAI cofounder Greg Brockman, Andreessen Horowitz, 8VC's Joe Lonsdale, SV Angel's Ron Conway, and Perplexity. The broader scale is corroborated by the Financial Times, which reported crypto and AI super PACs raising roughly $250 million ahead of the midterms and warned Democrats against angering an AI lobby it valued at around $300 million. In June 2026, Business Insider reported that OpenAI publicly distanced itself from Brockman's donations, stating that it does not direct Leading the Future's activities, has no visibility into its operations, has not donated to super PACs, and that AI advocacy groups should avoid tactics such as astroturfing.

Related reporting extended the picture beyond paid influencers. Model Republic reported that a site called The Wire by Acutus appeared to use AI-generated "reporters" to solicit comments from real people under false identities, with no masthead or named editors, and that a funding trail appeared to lead to OpenAI's political ecosystem. Tom's Hardware and The Verge picked up the same investigation, with Tom's Hardware reporting that a majority of the site's articles were flagged as machine-generated. In a separate piece, Model Republic alleged bot-like accounts on X coordinating engagement with Build American AI and Think Big PAC content. Model Republic is advocacy-adjacent and adversarial toward parts of the AI industry, so its claims carry less weight than the WIRED reporting and are treated here as leads with partial corroboration.

The counter-narrative emerged over the following weeks. The Bitcoin Policy Institute published "Foreign Influence in the Campaign against American AI" in mid-May and a second part, "The Singham Ground Game," on 29 June 2026. The reports argue that a foreign influence campaign, centered on Neville Roy Singham and the Party for Socialism and Liberation, runs parallel to authentic local concern about data centers. BPI's second part claims to document 21 PSL campaigns across 14 states that delayed, scaled back, or blocked approximately $23.6 billion in AI and data-center investment. The advocacy group Power the Future produced similar messaging.

The counter-narrative moved quickly into official channels. In early June, three GOP members of the House Energy and Commerce Committee requested briefings from the FBI and the President's science advisers on foreign efforts to block the data-center build-out, citing the BPI and Power the Future reports. Senator Tom Cotton urged the Justice Department to investigate a China-linked campaign he tied to Singham, and Representative John Moolenaar raised related concerns about Chinese manipulation of the US AI debate. WIRED reported that investor Kevin O'Leary used a BPI graphic in a video alleging foreign influence behind opposition to his Utah data-center project. Politico and NPR reporting, syndicated widely, described the theory of Chinese-funded data-center opposition circulating among Silicon Valley figures and in remarks by Interior Secretary Doug Burgum.

## Positions of the parties

**Pro-AI political infrastructure.** Leading the Future and Build American AI present themselves as advocates for American AI leadership against a Chinese competitive threat. Their public messaging emphasizes jobs, innovation, national security, and the risk of ceding the field to China, and largely avoids the contested local questions about energy, water, grid capacity, electricity prices, land use, and democratic consent.

**AI firms.** OpenAI and Palantir deny corporate involvement in the influencer campaign, and OpenAI has stated that it does not direct or fund Leading the Future and opposes astroturfing. This distancing means claims that "US AI firms were caught running influence operations" are not supported as stated. The accurate formulation is that AI-aligned political and advocacy infrastructure, backed by major tech figures and adjacent to leading firms, was exposed conducting these operations.

**Bitcoin Policy Institute.** BPI frames its work as documenting a foreign influence campaign that runs alongside genuine local concern. It acknowledges that Americans have real worries about data centers and that much local mobilization is ordinary civic activity, but argues that opaque funding and network overlaps point to China-linked direction. BPI openly advocates American leadership in compute infrastructure, and its own reporting notes that public records cannot establish whether China or foreign actors fund the campaigns it describes.

**Skeptical experts and analysts.** Graphika told WIRED it had not seen evidence of an organized or scaled influence operation traceable to a foreign actor, with limited exceptions, and said domestic US actors were leading the online anti-data-center conversation. OpenAI's own report found no meaningful breakout for the China-origin accounts it flagged. Independent coverage noted that a majority of data-center opposition is led by Republicans, which is difficult to reconcile with a primarily foreign-driven movement.

## Implications (second-order effects)

The most significant effect is the reframing of a domestic policy dispute as a national-security and counterintelligence matter. Once opposition to data centers is described as potential foreign influence, the response shifts from zoning boards and utility regulators toward federal law enforcement and congressional oversight. That shift raises the cost of ordinary civic opposition and creates a chilling risk for residents and local groups who object to specific projects on energy, water, or land-use grounds.

A second effect concerns the information environment itself. If the public comes to see pro-AI "grassroots" support as partly manufactured through paid influencers and automated media, the industry's claim to public backing weakens. The counter-narrative addresses that exposure by relocating the accusation of manufactured activity onto opponents. Whether or not this is coordinated, it is the practical function the BPI material performs in the current debate.

A third effect is the weaponization of genuine foreign-influence concern. Foreign actors do test and amplify domestic grievances, and OpenAI's report shows a real, if minor, China-origin attempt. The risk is that a legitimate monitoring concern is stretched into a general suspicion of a broad domestic movement, which degrades the credibility of foreign-influence analysis when it is most needed.

## Significance

This is a case of competing influence operations attached to a single high-value policy outcome, the pace and permitting of AI data-center construction. The pro-AI side has documented influence activity through paid creators and, on weaker evidence, automated media and bot amplification. The counter-narrative supplies an evidentiary-looking frame that shifts suspicion onto opponents. The structure matters more than any single actor. The influencer layer produces social reach, the alleged pseudo-news layer produces apparently independent articles, the PAC layer produces money and electoral pressure, and the policy-paper layer produces citations, graphics, and a national-security framing that officials and investors can cite.

The distinctive feature is that the counter-narrative operates largely through legitimate-looking institutions rather than covert accounts. A think-tank report is harder to dismiss than a bot network, which is precisely what makes it effective as a citation source and harder to attribute or rebut.

**Assessment.** BPI's material is very likely a functional component of a pro-AI counter-campaign rather than an independent finding, on the basis of narrative fit, timing, and downstream political use. It is not established as directly coordinated with or funded by the Build American AI or Leading the Future network. **Confidence: moderate to high on the functional interpretation; low on any claim of direct coordination.**

## Consensus and forecast outlook (Aunoo cross-source analysis)

News coverage of AI-related influence operations rose sharply through early June 2026 and peaked around 8 June, then fell away, on Aunoo's tracking of the reporting. Attention to this specific dispute is therefore past its peak as of early July, though the underlying policy fight over data centers continues. The tone of that coverage is strongly negative and has been growing more so, with the sharpest downward shift on 23 June and no sign yet of a reversal.

Across the wider body of reporting on disinformation and influence operations, coverage converges on two themes that frame this dispute. The first is the acceleration of AI-enabled disinformation. The second is the shift of organized influence work toward operating through legitimate platforms and influencer networks rather than crude propaganda. That second pattern, influence conducted through ostensibly independent intermediaries, is the same mechanism visible on both sides of the data-center dispute. Coverage of the specific China-influence claim splits along partisan lines: right-leaning outlets amplify the foreign-influence framing and the demands for federal investigation, while center and left-leaning outlets and specialist technology press foreground the weakness of the evidence and the domestic, largely Republican, character of the opposition.

## Outlook and indicators to watch

The near-term question is whether any federal agency acts on the demands for investigation. An FBI or DOJ inquiry framed around foreign influence, even a preliminary one, would substantially strengthen the counter-narrative regardless of what it eventually finds. A second indicator is the release of any specific, attributable evidence tying named US anti-data-center campaigns to foreign funding or direction; nothing in the current public record establishes this, and its appearance would materially change the assessment. A third indicator is further reporting on the pro-AI influence stack, particularly any documentation that connects the influencer campaign, the alleged pseudo-news layer, and the policy-paper layer through shared funding or contractors, which would move the functional interpretation toward a coordination claim. Finally, the midterm campaign is likely to intensify both narratives, since the underlying AI PAC money is explicitly aimed at the 2026 elections and federal AI regulation.

## Source assessment

The pro-AI influencer campaign rests on high-credibility direct reporting by WIRED, with named actors, campaign materials, creator outreach, and responses from implicated parties, and is treated here as well established. The funding context is corroborated by Axios and the Financial Times and is likewise strong. OpenAI's distancing statement is well sourced through Business Insider and limits corporate attribution. The automated pseudo-news and bot-amplification claims originate with Model Republic, an advocacy-adjacent outlet, and are corroborated in part by Tom's Hardware and The Verge for the Acutus investigation; they are treated as credible leads rather than settled fact, with the bot-amplification claim the weakest of the set.

On the counter-narrative, the existence and content of the BPI and Power the Future reports and their uptake by named lawmakers and investors are well documented across Aunoo's corpus, including the Washington Times, Fox News, Newsmax, PCMag, Gizmodo, TechRadar, Axios, and Politico and NPR syndication. The underlying causal claim that foreign influence drives anti-data-center opposition is not corroborated; the strongest contrary evidence comes from OpenAI's own report finding no meaningful breakout, WIRED's reporting of Graphika's assessment, and coverage noting the largely Republican composition of the opposition. The cited-versus-inferred boundary is important throughout: the timing and narrative fit between the exposure of the pro-AI campaign and the rise of the counter-narrative are consistent with a counter, but no source establishes direct coordination, and that link is inferred rather than reported.

**Confidence.** High on the pro-AI influencer campaign and its funding context, and on the existence and political uptake of the BPI counter-narrative. Moderate to high on the interpretation of BPI as a functional counter-narrative layer. Low on any claim of direct coordination between BPI and the pro-AI network, and low on the claim that anti-data-center opposition is materially foreign-driven. What would raise confidence: disclosure of funding or contractor links across the influence stack; release of attributable evidence on foreign funding of named campaigns; or a formal agency finding either way.
