Situation Report Updated 2 Jul 2026 Disinformation & Influence Operations

Pro-AI influence operations, and the counter-narrative that recasts data-center opposition as foreign influence

OSINT assessment compiled from named primary reporting and Aunoo news-intelligence
Bottom line up front

Two influence campaigns are running around the same fight over AI data centers. WIRED reported on 1 May 2026 that Build American AI, a dark-money group tied to the pro-AI super PAC Leading the Future, paid influencers to promote American AI and frame China as the threat. Reports from the Bitcoin Policy Institute and the group Power the Future then argued the reverse, that opposition to data centers is itself a China-linked foreign influence operation.

The evidence is not symmetric. The pro-AI influencer campaign is well documented. The claim that foreign actors drive anti-data-center opposition is not supported by the available evidence: OpenAI found no meaningful breakout for the China-origin activity it flagged, Graphika saw no scaled foreign operation, and a majority of the opposition is Republican-led. The BPI material is best interpreted as the policy-paper layer of a pro-AI counter-campaign, though no public evidence shows it is directly coordinated with that network.

What happened

WIRED documented a coordinated campaign in which Build American AI, tied to Leading the Future, paid lifestyle and political influencers, reportedly around $5,000 per TikTok video, to promote American AI and present China as the threat. The campaign sits in a large political-finance network: Axios reported Leading the Future raised more than $125 million, and the Financial Times sized the wider AI and crypto PAC environment at roughly $250 million to $300 million ahead of the midterms. OpenAI and Palantir deny corporate involvement, and OpenAI says it does not direct or fund Leading the Future and opposes astroturfing. Related reporting by Model Republic, corroborated in part by Tom's Hardware and The Verge, alleged an automated pseudo-news site staffed by AI "reporters" and bot-like amplification on X; those claims are treated here as credible leads rather than settled fact.

The counter-narrative

Beginning in mid-May, the Bitcoin Policy Institute published "Foreign Influence in the Campaign against American AI," followed by "The Singham Ground Game" on 29 June, arguing that opposition to data centers runs parallel to a China-linked campaign centered on Neville Roy Singham and the Party for Socialism and Liberation, and claiming roughly $23.6 billion in blocked investment. The framing moved quickly into official channels: House Energy and Commerce members, Senator Tom Cotton, and Representative John Moolenaar cited the reports to seek FBI and DOJ investigations, and investor Kevin O'Leary used a BPI graphic to allege foreign influence behind opposition to his own data-center project.

Why this matters

Venue shift. The counter-narrative recasts a local dispute about energy, water, land, and electricity prices as a national-security and counterintelligence matter, moving it toward the FBI, DOJ, and congressional oversight and raising the cost of ordinary civic opposition.
Relocated accusation. After the pro-AI campaign was exposed, the counter-narrative moves the charge of manufactured activity onto opponents. That is the practical function the BPI material performs, whether or not it is coordinated.
Weaponized concern. Foreign actors do test domestic grievances, and OpenAI found a real if minor attempt. The risk is stretching a legitimate monitoring concern into blanket suspicion of a broad domestic movement.
Where this sits in the Aunoo foresight

On Aunoo's tracking of the news coverage, reporting on AI-related influence operations peaked around 8 June and has since declined, so attention to this specific dispute is past its peak while the underlying data-center fight continues. The tone of that coverage is strongly negative and growing more so, with the sharpest shift on 23 June and no reversal in sight. Across reporting on influence operations more broadly, coverage converges on one mechanism in particular: influence conducted through legitimate-looking intermediaries rather than crude propaganda, which is what is visible on both sides of this dispute.

What to watch

  • Federal action — whether any agency opens an inquiry; even a preliminary one would strengthen the counter-narrative regardless of its findings.
  • Attributable evidence — any documentation tying named US campaigns to foreign funding, none of which exists in the public record so far.
  • Stack linkage — reporting connecting the influencer, pseudo-news, and policy-paper layers through shared money or contractors.
  • Midterm intensification — the AI PAC money is aimed at the 2026 elections and federal AI regulation, so both narratives are likely to sharpen.
