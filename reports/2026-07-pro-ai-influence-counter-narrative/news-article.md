# The pro-AI lobby was caught paying influencers. Now it says the backlash is a Chinese plot.

*Articles · Disinformation, AI/ML, Media Literacy · 2 July 2026 · 7–9 minutes*

---

There are two influence campaigns running around America's fight over AI data centers, and they point in opposite directions. One is well documented. The other is mostly an accusation. The gap between them is the story.

In May, WIRED reported that a dark-money group was **[paying social-media influencers to talk up American AI and cast China as the threat](https://www.wired.com/story/super-pac-backed-by-openai-and-palantir-is-paying-tiktok-influencers-to-fear-monger-about-china/)**. Over the following weeks, a set of reports argued almost the reverse: that the people objecting to data centers in their towns were themselves part of a China-linked foreign influence operation. Lawmakers picked up the second claim and asked the FBI and the Justice Department to investigate. What the evidence actually supports is worth spelling out, because the two sides are not on equal footing.

### What was documented

The paid-influencer campaign has names attached to it. WIRED reported that Build American AI, a nonprofit tied to the pro-AI super PAC Leading the Future, ran a coordinated push using lifestyle and political creators, with influencers reportedly offered around $5,000 per TikTok video to promote American AI and frame China as the danger. The money behind that world is large. Axios reported that Leading the Future had **[raised more than $125 million](https://www.axios.com/2026/01/30/openai-a16z-cash-ai-super-pac)** to shape the midterms and federal AI rules, with backers including OpenAI cofounder Greg Brockman and the venture firm Andreessen Horowitz, and the Financial Times has put the wider AI and crypto PAC environment at **[roughly $250 million](https://www.ft.com/content/addbcf9e-d352-417b-af1c-ab1acae35771)** heading into the elections.

The companies themselves keep their distance. OpenAI and Palantir deny corporate involvement, and OpenAI has said it does not direct or fund Leading the Future and **[opposes tactics like astroturfing](https://www.businessinsider.com/openai-greg-brockman-political-donations-super-pac-statement-leading-future-2026-6)**. That distinction matters. The accurate way to describe what was exposed is a pro-AI political and advocacy machine backed by major tech figures, not the AI firms acting as corporations. There are also thinner threads around the same network, including reporting that an automated news site staffed by AI "reporters" was used to gather quotes and that bot-like accounts amplified pro-AI posts. Those claims come from an advocacy-leaning outlet and are best treated as leads rather than proven facts.

### What is only alleged

The counter-story arrived through think-tank reports. The Bitcoin Policy Institute published two installments arguing that opposition to data centers runs alongside a China-linked campaign tied to the businessman Neville Roy Singham, and claimed that this activity had blocked roughly $23.6 billion in AI investment. That framing moved fast. Republican members of the House Energy and Commerce Committee asked for FBI briefings, Senator Tom Cotton **[urged the Justice Department to investigate](https://www.foxnews.com/politics/sen-tom-cotton-urges-doj-probe-chinese-bid-kneecap-american-ai)**, and the investor Kevin O'Leary used one of the reports' graphics to blame foreign influence for opposition to his own data-center project.

The problem is that the central claim is not supported by the evidence. OpenAI's own threat report found a **[likely China-based operation testing anti-data-center messaging](https://cyberscoop.com/openai-china-influence-campaign-chatgpt/)**, but said it saw no evidence the effort broke through to real audiences. WIRED reported that the analysis firm Graphika had not found a scaled foreign operation, and that the online conversation was being led by Americans. One outlet noted the obvious tension in the theory: a **[majority of data-center opposition is led by Republicans](https://www.techradar.com/pro/republicans-call-on-fbi-to-investigate-anti-data-center-sentiment-as-a-chinese-psyop-despite-55-percent-of-data-center-opposition-headed-by-republicans)**, which is hard to reconcile with a movement supposedly driven from Beijing. NPR and others documented the theory **[catching on among Silicon Valley figures](https://www.wesanews.org/national-international-news/2026-06-10/the-theory-taking-the-rich-by-storm-china-funds-data-center-haters)** despite the lack of direct proof.

### Why the frame matters

The reframing does real work. Data-center fights are usually about electricity prices, water use, land, and who bears the local cost of a national buildout. Recast opposition as possible foreign influence, and the venue changes: instead of zoning boards and utility regulators, the matter heads toward the FBI, the Justice Department, and congressional oversight. That raises the cost of ordinary objection for residents who simply do not want a data center next door.

It also arrives at a convenient moment. Once the public learns that some of the "grassroots" enthusiasm for AI was paid for, the industry's claim to popular support looks weaker. The counter-narrative answers that problem by moving the charge of manufactured activity onto the other side. Whether or not it was coordinated, that is what the think-tank material does in practice: it supplies a credible-looking citation that officials and investors can point to. No public evidence shows the Bitcoin Policy Institute is funded by or coordinated with the pro-AI PAC network, so the honest description is that it functions as the policy-paper layer of a counter-campaign, not that it is a proven part of one.

None of this means foreign interference is imaginary. States do test and amplify real domestic grievances, and OpenAI found a small example of exactly that. The risk is the opposite error: stretching a genuine, minor signal into a blanket suspicion of a broad and largely homegrown movement. That erodes the credibility of foreign-influence warnings for the moment they are actually needed.

### What to watch

- **Whether any agency opens an inquiry.** Even a preliminary one would boost the counter-narrative regardless of what it eventually finds.
- **Any real evidence of foreign funding.** Nothing in the public record ties named US campaigns to foreign money; that would change the picture.
- **Links across the pro-AI stack.** Reporting that connects the paid influencers, the AI-written news site, and the policy reports through shared money would turn a functional interpretation into a coordination story.
